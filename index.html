Part I

The Master Plan

The business, the land, the money, and the phased path from square one.

The Vision

A storybook farm, planned like an engineer.

The dream is cozy: round doors, green roofs, lantern-lit lanes, pizza and a pint by the fire. The plan behind it is rigorous: a measured 30-acre grid where every square is assigned a use, a surface, a planting, and a price. This document leaves nothing to the imagination, from which fig tree goes where to which cobblestone to buy and from whom.

Measured, not sketched

The whole farm is drawn on a 100 ft by 100 ft grid (each square is 0.23 acre). Every zone has grid coordinates, a real area, and a reason it sits where it does.

Costed to the cobblestone

Plants, seed, trees, pavers, gravel, fencing, buildings: each line has a current price, a quantity, and a named vendor for the best deal near Raleigh.

Homes kept separate

The 30 business acres carry no houses. The 5 to 6 family homesteads sit on their own ~2-acre lots on a separate parcel, which also keeps the farm's tax status clean.

The guiding strategy

Plant the slow things first (trees, vines, bees, soil). Earn money early from cheap, high-draw farm events. Build the cozy, expensive structures as the farm pays for them. The lazy river, the most expensive and permit-heavy piece, is parked as a reserved future phase: we protect its corridor on the map but build it last, long after we are open and profitable.

The Look & The Science

An Earth-Sheltered Hamlet

Tucking the buildings into the hills under green roofs, and paving the lanes in permeable cobblestone, solves three hard problems at once: it slashes energy cost, it satisfies Wake's watershed limits, and it looks like nowhere else in North Carolina.

Geo-insulation pays

NC ground holds a steady ~60°F. Earth-sheltering plus green roofs cut heating and cooling load by half or more. For the brewery this is gold: a hillside cellar that holds cellar temperature means cheaper, steadier fermentation. Pair with a geothermal heat pump (30% federal credit).

The look is a loophole

Under NC rules, "built-upon area" only counts surfaces water cannot pass through. Green roofs and permeable cobblestone count as pervious, so the Shire look lets us build far more on a watershed-capped parcel than asphalt and shingles would.

Humid clay is hard mode

Done wrong it means damp and mold. Done right it is wonderful. Non-negotiables: true exterior waterproofing, drainage boards, footing drains, granular backfill, mechanical dehumidification, and radon mitigation. Reinforced concrete or ICF shells, never wood frame. Budget a 10 to 20% premium.

Where We Live

The Homestead Commons

The 30 business acres carry no houses, on purpose: it keeps the bona fide farm status clean and keeps homes out of the event noise and traffic. The 5 to 6 family homes sit on their own ~2-acre lots on a separate parcel, sharing a private lane, a short walk or golf-cart ride from the farm.

Reality check: this is a real subdivision, not a quick family split

NC has no "transfer-to-family" subdivision shortcut. Carving 5 to 6 lots is a full minor or major subdivision: plat approval, a built private road, and an individual perc test, well, and septic for every lot. Budget the time and the infrastructure.

Watershed overlays can force bigger lots. In Wake's critical watershed areas the density cap is about 1 home per 2 acres (R-80W); parts of Chatham near rivers require 1 home per 5 acres. A creek bonus on the parcel can quietly cut how many homes you are allowed, so check the zoning and the watershed class together before counting on six lots.

The layout

  • 5 to 6 lots at ~2 acres each = 10 to 12 acres of homesites.
  • A shared private gravel lane (~1,000 ft) with a common entrance off the road.
  • Each lot: a home envelope, its own well and septic, a driveway, and a buffer to the neighbor.
  • Earth-sheltered hill-homes here too, if the family wants the look, on the same green-roof principles as the farm.
  • A shared common: a fire pit green, a garden, maybe a guest "great room."

The total land math

  • Business core: 30 acres
  • Homestead Commons (6 lots + lane + buffer): ~14 acres
  • Creek buffers, slopes, unbuildable: ~3 to 8 acres
  • Target parcel to acquire: 45 to 55 acres. This is why we now shop for 45+ acre listings, not 30.

How we keep it all alive

Orchard & vines

Figs and Asian persimmons are nearly no-spray. Apples (Liberty/Enterprise) are scab- and fireblight-resistant, the low-spray stars; plant pollinizer pairs. Drip irrigate, mulch rings, prune in late winter, and protect young trunks from deer and string trimmers.

Berries & lavender

Blueberries demand acidic, well-drained raised beds (sulfur + peat). Strawberries are an annual plasticulture crop replanted each fall. Lavender is the diva: gravelly raised berms, lime to near-neutral, full sun and airflow, and a budget to replace losses.

Fields & meadow

Succession-sow sunflowers every 10 to 14 days for staggered bloom. The meadow is all in the site prep: kill the existing weeds for a season first, then dormant-seed in late fall. Cover crops (rye + clover) build the clay between cash crops.

The growing year

Winter

Plant bare-root trees and blueberries, prune, lime, set strawberry beds, dormant-seed the meadow, file EQIP, start tomato seed indoors.

Spring

After ~April 8 frost: transplant tomatoes, peppers, basil; sow sunflowers and corn; bees build up; strawberry U-pick opens.

Summer

Harvest tomatoes, berries, figs; pull honey; sunflower fields bloom; sow pumpkins; keep drip running through the heat.

Fall

Pumpkin + corn-maze season (the revenue peak); plant garlic and fall greens; seed fescue lawns; sow cover crops.

The cobblestone strategy

Use Belgard Aqua-Bric permeable pavers (~$6–10/sf installed) for the main lanes and plazas, real granite setts only at the signature entry and taproom plaza where guests linger, and decomposed granite ($1–3/sf) for the quiet back paths. Budget ~20,000 sf of lanes at a blended ~$6/sf installed = roughly $120,000 at full build, phased.

The Land & Real Listings

We now need 45 to 55 acres (30 for the farm, ~14 for the homesteads, plus creek buffer), so the search has moved to 45+ acre tracts. Raleigh is the seat of Wake County; eastern Wake is the "stay in Wake" target, with value tracts in the adjacent commuter counties.

The master key: become a bona fide farm first

The brewery, taproom, and paid lodging are not exempt and need Special Use Permits, but farm status is the single most powerful move. Keeping the homes on a separate parcel protects this status.

Watershed: friend and foe

Wake overlays (Falls Lake, Swift Creek, Jordan, Little River) cap impervious surface and can force 2 to 5 acre minimum home lots. Our green roofs and permeable cobblestone beat the impervious cap, but the lot-size rule still limits the homestead count. Check the parcel's watershed class and the impervious cap together.

Can we dig & build?

Cecil and Appling soils (central/east Wake) perc well, hold foundations, and are low-shrink, good for earth-sheltering. Avoid the SW Wake Triassic basin (White Store/Creedmoor clays) and watch for shallow granite in NE Wake. A creek means a 50-ft no-build buffer each side. Get a soil scientist and geotech on any shortlisted parcel.

The value play

If budget binds, a creek tract in Johnston (Clayton/Four Oaks), Franklin (Louisburg), or Granville (Oxford/Stem) gives the same Raleigh customer base, lighter zoning friction, and land for roughly half the in-Wake price, the single biggest line item cut by a third or more.

The Plumbing of the Dream

Utilities, Water & Septic

The least glamorous, most important page. A rural site has no city water or sewer, so wastewater capacity quietly caps the number of seats, taps, and beds, which caps the whole business.

Get this study before you buy any parcel

A restaurant uses ~40 gallons of wastewater per seat per day. Add a brewery and events and the site blows past ~3,000 gallons a day, which means a state-reviewed, engineer-designed advanced wastewater system (drip/LPP + pretreatment) and likely a regulated public water system (treatment, certified operator, testing). Hire a soil scientist and PE for a wastewater feasibility study before you make an offer. The answer sizes the whole business.

Wastewater (the cap)

  • Early phases: conventional septic for the farm stand, staff, and event trailers.
  • Full build: PE-designed advanced system, state-permitted, reserve drainfield set aside.
  • Brewery wastewater is high-strength; spent grain feeds animals, not the drain.
  • Site beats equipment: Cecil/Appling perc, Triassic clays do not.

Water & green roofs

  • One ag well covers early farming and irrigation.
  • Serving the public means a public water system permit; plan multiple wells.
  • Brewing uses ~7 gallons of water per gallon of beer.
  • Green roofs soak up 60 to 80% of their rain; ponds + rainwater handle irrigation.

Energy & the rest

  • Geothermal heat pumps fit NC clay and the earth-sheltered shells (30% commercial credit).
  • Solar via USDA REAP; backup generator for the coolers.
  • Confirm three-phase power with Wake Electric or Duke early.
  • Propane for the oven/heat; fiber or Starlink for POS and bookings.
Earth-sheltered = moisture discipline

Exterior waterproofing, drainage boards, footing drains, granular backfill, mandatory mechanical dehumidification, and a radon system (Wake is EPA Zone 2). Size each utility to the current phase and stub conduit and water toward future cottages now while the trenches are open, so later phases plug in instead of digging twice.

The Architecture

How the buildings look and how they are built into the hill.

The Enchanting Brief

Six moves that make it magic

"Enchanting" is not an accident; it is a set of deliberate architectural decisions, each borrowed from a building that already works. These six principles govern every drawing that follows.

The earth is the architecture

Like the Vetsch earth houses in Switzerland, the building is a shell blanketed in soil and meadow. From the hill behind, it nearly disappears; from the valley it glows. The ground itself holds it at a steady ~60°F.

Compression, then release

Frank Lloyd Wright's trick: enter through a low, snug, lantern-lit threshold (~7 ft) that suddenly opens into a soaring trussed hall (16 to 24 ft). The hall feels twice its true size because of the squeeze before it.

One great hearth

From the Green Dragon Inn to every Cotswold pub: a single massive double-sided stone inglenook with benches built into it. A live fire is the heart of the room and the first thing you see.

Light as the magic

Therme Vals proves daylight in a buried building comes from thin, deliberate slots, not big walls of glass. A glazed south face, clerestory monitors, and a sunken court drop blades of light into stone and timber.

Prospect and refuge

Every booth and snug is enclosed on three sides with your back to a wall (refuge) and a view of the room and the fire (prospect). It is why a good nook feels safe and a bad table feels exposed.

Real materials only

Hobbiton reads as magic because the brick, stone, and thatch are real and weathering. We use NC granite, white oak, lime render, copper, and a living roof, never plastic or paint pretending to be them.

The Magic Layer

Light: How It Becomes Enchanting at Dusk

More than any single material, light is what turns the hamlet magical after sunset. The whole property runs warm, dim, and shielded, so every round window glows like a lantern and the buildings read as embers in the hillside.

Interior

  • 2700K everywhere, 2200–2400K for accents (near candlelight), never above 3000K indoors.
  • CRI ≥ 90 so wood, copper, skin, and beer all look rich.
  • Dim-to-warm fixtures that drop to 2200K as they dim, so the room "goes golden" through the evening.
  • Layered pools from lanterns, sconces, and filament pendants, never flat overhead light.
  • The copper brewhouse and the hearth lit as the two glowing anchors.

Exterior & dark-sky

  • Wrought-iron lanterns with seeded glass and candle-style lamps flank every round door.
  • Path lights 8 to 10 ft apart (4 to 5 ft at steps); step lights just 20 to 40 lumens tucked under the tread.
  • Uplight the feature tree (2 to 3 fixtures, 45 to 60°) and graze the stone walls to reveal texture.
  • A dimmed warm light just inside each round window turns it into a glowing eye.
  • Fully shielded, ≤2700K, on dimmers and timers, dark-sky compliant, which is also exactly the cozy look.
The single rule

Warm, dim, shielded, and aimed at things (not the sky). Restraint is the whole trick: a few glowing lanterns and a tree read as magic, while a parking-lot blaze of cool floodlight kills it instantly. The same restraint that makes it enchanting makes it dark-sky friendly and cheap to run.

How It Gets Built

The Earth-Sheltered Construction Sequence

Building into a hill is a specific order of operations. Get it right, especially the waterproofing and the bracing-before-backfill, and it lasts a century; get it wrong and it leaks. The big pieces need a stamped engineer; the sequence is the same at any scale.

1 · Cut the slope

Geotech borings first. Bench the south-facing hillside, hold back the cut, and shape the building pad and the future berm grades.

2 · Footings & slab

Engineered footings; sub-slab gravel, radon vent loop, vapor barrier, rigid insulation, then the slab (thickened under brewery tanks).

3 · Raise the shell

Reinforced-concrete or ICF retaining walls; set the precast double-tee roof (or vault). The shell is the structure that holds back the earth.

4 · Waterproof everything

The make-or-break step: continuous hydrostatic membrane on walls and roof, drainage board, and footing drains run to daylight downslope.

5 · Insulate & roof

Rigid insulation outboard of the waterproofing, then the living-roof buildup: root barrier, drainage, filter fabric, growing medium, sedum.

6 · Brace, then backfill

Only after the floor and roof brace the walls, backfill with clean granular stone and build the native berm at 3:1, with swales diverting surface water.

7 · Systems & fit-out

Geothermal, the dedicated dehumidification, radon fan, MEP, sprinklers; then the warm finishes: timber, lime plaster, brick floors, the hearth.

8 · Plant it in

Establish the sedum roof and the berm meadow, run the cobblestone up to the round door, hang the lanterns. The building disappears into the hill.

Before any of this is built

The retaining walls and the earth-covered roof span are beyond prescriptive code and must be designed and stamped by a North Carolina structural engineer, on top of a geotechnical report, a licensed architect, and Wake County permits. Earth-sheltered building rewards precision and punishes shortcuts, especially on waterproofing and humidity control. Build a test mock-up of the wall assembly, and budget the active radon fan and the dehumidification from day one.

Hand This to the Builder

Wall-Assembly Mock-up Spec

Before the real walls go up, build one small test panel of the full earth-shelter assembly and prove it does not leak or sweat. A few thousand dollars here prevents a six-figure mistake. This is the one-page spec for that mock-up.

Why a mock-up

Earth-sheltered buildings fail at the details, not the concept: a lapped membrane, a clogged drainage board, a cold surface that condenses. A mock-up lets you flood-test the waterproofing, check the drainage, and confirm the dew-point math before the building exists, when fixes are cheap.

Build this panel

  • An 8 ft x 8 ft section of bermed wall plus a ~6 ft roof return with the full living-roof stack, including one inside corner and one membrane lap.
  • Real structural backing (ICF or concrete), the actual specified membrane, drainage board, insulation, and growing medium.
  • A working footing drain daylighted to a visible outlet, and a temporary interior face to read surface temperature and humidity.

Acceptance tests

  • Flood / spray test the waterproofing 24-48 hrs; zero moisture at the interior face.
  • Drainage: water poured at the roof exits the footing drain within minutes, runs clear.
  • Membrane laps + penetrations pass adhesion and probe inspection.
  • Dew-point check: interior surface stays 3-5°F above dewpoint with the dehumidifier holding 50-55% RH.
  • Root barrier intact after media + planting; no root strike at the membrane.
